Odyssey - Filter Equalization

The filter response is sometimes found to depend on the position of an object in the field of view. This depends essentially on two factors: astygmatism in the optical system and lighting inhomogeneities.

Astygmatism is sometimes detectable by eye. A grain image in the center of the field of view looks much sharper than when it is far from the center. Of course, part of this effect depends on spherical aberration (so the focus varies from the center to the edges), but if spherical aberration were the only cause, a careful refocusing would yield a sharp grain image; indeed, grains far from the center can never yield sharp images, and this is called astygmatism: the image of a point is not a point, but a region with a shape that depends on the specific optical equipment of the microscope, and on the position in the field of view. In this conditions, the FIR filter response peak too has an height that depends on the position of the grain in the field of view.

Lighting inhomogeneities, when very strong, can severely affect the filter performances. Indeed, FIR filters are linear filters. This means that the dynamic range of the filter output is proportional to the dynamic range of the input signal. Of course, the information content of a well illuminated area is larger than what a poorly illuminated area can provide; the filter peaks will then be higher in the illuminated areas.

Since the filter output is to be binarized, for each pixel the interesting information is just whether the filter output reaches or not a certain level. Thus, adding a pedestal image to the filter output can help in most cases to recover the effects of astygmatism and incorrect lighting. The pedestal image varies very much, so Odyssey provides no predefined utility to make such images. External algorithms that provide these images should tailor them very carefully on the specific case.
